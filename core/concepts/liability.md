## In one sentence (citizen)
If the artificial company causes harm, the law must always find someone to answer for it. First
the company pays with its own assets; behind it stands a named responsible party.

## In practice (professional)
Layered allocation: the entity's patrimony answers first; declared operators and beneficial owners
stand behind it under defined conditions; insurance and piercing rules close residual gaps. The
registry (`concept-0003`) makes each layer traceable.

## Formal definition (researcher)
The allocation regime mapping harms caused by an artificial legal person to answerable parties and
assets, designed so the causal-legal chain from act to accountable party is never broken.

## Example
Argentina's proposed *sociedad automatizada* answers for damages caused by its autonomous systems
with its own assets.

## Counterexample
"The robbery without the robber": harm occurs, profits were collected, and no identifiable party
can be made to answer — the failure mode this concept exists to prevent.

## Frequently misunderstood
Limited liability is not "no liability". It bounds financial exposure; it does not erase the duty
to answer, nor the traceability of who stands behind the entity.

## Practical implications
Liability design is the single factor that decides whether artificial legal personhood is a safe
institution or a loophole.
